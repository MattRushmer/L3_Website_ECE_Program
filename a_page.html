Education First

Age Group: 3-5 years old
Duration: 45-60 minutes
Theme: The Letter "A"


Educational Goals:
• Children will recognize the letter "A" in uppercase and lowercase form.
• Children will be able to identify words that begin with the letter "A."
• Children will learn the sound of the letter "A" and its phonetic use.
• Children will practice writing the letter "A."
• Children will engage in fine and gross motor activities related to the letter "A."

1. Introduction to the Letter "A"

Objective: Introduce the letter "A" visually and phonetically to children, helping them associate the letter with its sound and objects that start with it.

Materials Needed
• Flashcards showing the uppercase and lowercase letter "A."
• Pictures of items that start with the letter "A" (such as apple, ant, alligator, airplane, etc.).
• A large poster or chart with the letter "A" written in bold, colorful print.
• Optional: Plastic letters, or letter cut-outs for tactile engagement.

Activity:
 • Step 1: Start by showing the children the flashcards with the uppercase "A" and lowercase "a" (alternatively, use large visual posters). Hold up each card slowly and clearly pronounce the letter. Repeat the sound of the letter together ("Aaaah").
• Step 2: Explain the shape of the letter. Point to the vertical line, diagonal lines, and the horizontal line in uppercase "A." This step helps children understand the structure.
• Step 3: Associate the letter "A" with various objects that start with it. Show pictures or real objects and say, "This is an apple, apple starts with A. A is for apple!" Repeat for other objects like an ant, alligator, airplane, and others that are common and recognizable to young children. Make sure to say the words slowly and clearly.
• Step 4: Have the children repeat after you as they point to the pictures, practicing both the letter sound and the word.

Tip: Encourage children to point to the pictures as you say the words to reinforce the connection between the visual letter and the object.



2. Sensory Activity: Tracing the Letter "A" in Sand/Rice

Objective: Provide a tactile experience where children trace the letter "A," reinforcing its shape and sound through sensory play.

Materials Needed
• A shallow tray filled with sand, rice, or salt (about 1-2 inches deep).
• A small wooden stick or finger for tracing.
• Flashcards or a large letter "A" as a model.

Activity:
 • Step 1: Show the children the large letter "A" flashcard again and demonstrate how to trace the letter with your finger in the sand or rice, saying the letter sound ("Aaaah") as you trace.
• Step 2: Invite the children to take turns tracing the letter "A" in the sand or rice with their fingers or a stick. Encourage them to say the sound of the letter as they trace it. If needed, gently guide their hands to show them the proper motion (top-to-bottom for uppercase, a simple line for lowercase).
• Step 3: Ask the children to trace the letter multiple times, providing positive reinforcement for their attempts. Allow them to experiment by creating their own "A" shapes and practicing until they feel comfortable.

Tip: For children who may not yet be able to trace accurately, encourage them to make marks and enjoy the process of exploration rather than perfection.



3. Story Time: Letter "A" in a Storybook

Objective: Help children connect the letter "A" with a narrative and introduce the letter in a contextual setting through storytelling.

Materials Needed
• A picture book about the letter "A" or one with words beginning with "A" (e.g., The Apple Tree by Nadia Alramly or Annie's Adventures).
• A comfortable space for reading (floor cushions, small reading area).

Activity:
 • Step 1: Gather the children in a circle and read aloud a story that includes the letter "A" or words that start with "A." If the book features large, colorful pictures, point to each image as you read.
• Step 2: As you read, pause occasionally to emphasize the "A" words (e.g., apple, ant, alligator) and encourage children to say them with you. Ask children to listen for words that begin with the letter "A" and raise their hands when they hear one.
• Step 3: After reading, have a brief discussion with the children. Ask them to recall some of the "A" words they heard in the story. Encourage them to think of any other "A" words they can remember.

Tip: Use animated voices for the characters to keep the children engaged. Encourage questions and interactions throughout the story.



4. Art & Craft: Apple Print Art

Objective: Reinforce recognition of the letter "A" by combining it with creative play and hands-on art.

Materials Needed
• Red, green, or yellow paint.
• An apple (cut in half horizontally).
• Large sheets of white paper.
• Aprons or old shirts to protect clothing.
• Optional: Crayons or markers for additional decoration.

Activity:
 • Step 1: Show the children how to dip the flat side of the apple half into the paint, covering it evenly.
• Step 2: Press the apple onto the paper to create a print. Explain how the apple starts with the letter "A" and invite the children to make their own prints.
• Step 3: Once the prints are made, encourage the children to decorate around their apple prints, drawing seeds, leaves, or even adding the letter "A" beside their artwork.

Tip: Encourage the children to focus on the process rather than perfection. If some prints don’t turn out exactly as expected, emphasize creativity and exploration!

5. Movement Activity: "A" Dance Party

Objective: Encourage physical movement and gross motor skills while reinforcing the letter "A" through play.

Materials Needed
• Music (upbeat, child-friendly songs).
• Open space for dancing and movement.

Activity:
 • Step 1: Play music and invite the children to stand up and move around the room. Explain that they will be dancing like animals and objects that start with "A."
• Step 2: Call out different "A" words and demonstrate how to move like those objects or animals. For example:
o Move like an "ant" (small, quick movements).
o Dance like an "alligator" (slithering or snapping movements).
o Pretend to fly like an "airplane" (arms outstretched like wings).
o Hop like a "kangaroo" (even though it’s not technically an "A" word, it’s fun and helps with motor skills).
• Step 3: Let the children take turns calling out an "A" word, and others will copy the movement.

Tip: Incorporate pauses in the music, allowing the children to freeze and hold a pose when the music stops. This helps develop control over their bodies.



6. Letter "A" Recognition & Writing Practice

Objective: Support children in practicing writing the letter "A," both uppercase and lowercase.

Materials Needed
• Worksheets with dotted lines to guide children in tracing the letter "A."
• Pencils, crayons, or markers.

Activity:
• Step 1: Show children how to properly hold a pencil. Demonstrate writing the letter "A" on a whiteboard or chart paper.
• Step 2: Give each child a worksheet with dotted lines forming the letter "A." Encourage them to trace the lines with their pencils, saying the sound of "A" as they trace.
• Step 3: Once they are comfortable tracing, ask them to try writing the letter "A" on their own. Offer guidance as necessary.

Tip: For younger children, use large lined paper with plenty of space for them to practice without feeling overwhelmed.



7. Snack & Discussion Time: Apple Tasting

Objective: Encourage children to use their senses while learning more about the letter "A."

Materials Needed
• Sliced apples.
• Napkins or small plates for serving.

Activity:
 • Step 1: Provide each child with a slice of apple. Discuss how the apple starts with the letter "A."
• Step 2: As children enjoy the apple, ask them questions like, "What does it taste like?" or "What color is your apple?" to encourage sensory exploration.
• Step 3: Encourage children to think of other foods that start with "A" and share ideas (apricots, almonds, etc.).

Tip: You can also introduce a quick "Apple Songs" activity, where children sing a simple song about apples while snacking.



8. Closing Circle

Objective: Review and reinforce the learning of the letter "A" through an interactive closing session.

Materials Needed
• Letter "A" flashcards.

Activity:
 • Step 1: Gather the children in a circle and review the day's activities. Hold up flashcards of the letter "A" and ask the children to identify it.
• Step 2: Ask the children to share one word that starts with "A" (such as apple, ant, or alligator). Use this opportunity to reinforce the sound and shape of the letter.
• Step 3: Sing the "A is for apple" song together or create a new short song that highlights words beginning with "A."



Assessment & Reflection

• Observation: Pay attention to whether children are able to identify the letter "A," recognize its sound, and name words that start with it.
• Engagement: After the program, ask the children what their favorite activity was and why, to gauge their engagement and enjoyment.
